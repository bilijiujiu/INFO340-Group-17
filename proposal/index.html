JobTrack: A Job Hunting Visualization and Management Tool

Problem Description

For many college students and recent graduates, job hunting is not only about finding job postings. It also involves managing a large amount of information, including deadlines, application status, interview progress, job descriptions, contact details, and follow-up tasks. In reality, this information is often spread across multiple places such as LinkedIn, Handshake, email, spreadsheets, calendars, and personal notes. Because the process is fragmented, students can easily miss deadlines, forget important details, or struggle to understand how their overall job search is going.

Some existing tools already try to support parts of this process. Handshake helps students search for internships and jobs, while LinkedIn provides features like saved jobs and a job tracker. Other tools such as Huntr also help users organize applications, notes, and deadlines. However, these tools mainly focus on job discovery or basic tracking rather than helping users clearly visualize the bigger picture of their search. This creates an information problem: students may have access to opportunities, but they still lack a simple way to organize, interpret, and reflect on their full job search process. Our project addresses this gap by exploring how a web app can combine management and visualization to help users feel more organized and in control.

App Description

Our proposed app is a job hunting platform designed mainly for college students who are looking for internships, part-time jobs, and full-time jobs. Our primary users are students who often feel overwhelmed by scattered job information and unclear job requirements. The app helps them organize opportunities they find, track their progress through every stage of applying, and reflect on their patterns over time to make smarter decisions about where to focus their energy. When users first sign up, they complete a short onboarding form that captures their preferences: preferred job type, target locations, salary range, education level, and visa sponsorship needs. This information personalizes the experience by filtering relevant job opportunities and contextualizing the analytics they see later. Users can update these preferences anytime in Settings. The data in our app comes from two sources: information users enter themselves (saved jobs, application status, notes, tasks, onboarding preferences) and the metadata of the job opportunities they save (company, role, salary, location, deadline, source). All of this is organized into three core feature areas.

Key Features

  • Job Tracking & Filtering: Users add jobs to a kanban-style board with five status columns: Saved, Applied, Interview, Offer, and Rejected. Each job card stores company name, role, salary, location, deadline, source link, and free-form notes. Users can move cards between columns as their application progresses, filter the board by job type or company, and add new jobs through an "+ Add Job" form that takes title, company, salary, deadline, location, and source URL.
  • Application Workspace: Each job opens into a dedicated workspace where users record everything related to that specific application. The workspace includes a notes area for personal impressions and reminders, a status selector to update where the application stands, and a task checklist for follow-up actions like sending thank-you emails, completing coding challenges, or doing mock interview prep. Users can add custom tasks for any job, turning "job hunting" into an actionable schedule rather than a vague to-do list, addressing the reality that job hunting itself is a job.
  • Analytics & Insight: The analytics page summarizes patterns across all of a user's applications. It shows aggregate KPIs (total applications, interview rate, offer rate, jobs viewed), a status distribution pie chart that reveals where applications get stuck, a breakdown of applications by required education level, top locations the user is targeting, and the share of saved jobs that sponsor visas. Crucially, each chart includes an automatically-generated insight caption that interprets the data. For example, "36% of your applications are stuck at the Interview stage — consider following up," or "Most saved jobs require a Master's — try filtering for roles that match your education level." This turns raw data into actionable guidance, so users don't just see their numbers but understand what to do next.

The app reduces the stress and confusion of job hunting by consolidating opportunities into one place and surfacing patterns the user couldn't see across spreadsheets and bookmarks — making the process more manageable for students juggling coursework, research, and the search itself.
